Understanding bipolar disorder

Not just mood swings.
A full shift in how you experience reality.

Bipolar disorder involves distinct episodes that can last days to months — not the everyday emotional fluctuations everyone feels. Each phase has its own texture, and recognizing them is the first step toward care.

During a manic episode, a person may feel invincible, sleep very little without feeling tired, think and speak rapidly, make impulsive decisions, or experience grandiosity. It can feel extraordinary — even euphoric — before it becomes overwhelming.

During a depressive episode, the weight can be immobilizing. Profound sadness, inability to concentrate, loss of interest in things once loved, and, in some cases, thoughts of self-harm or suicide.

Between episodes, many people function well. Bipolar disorder is not constant chaos — it's a pattern that, when understood, can be anticipated and managed.

Mania

Elevated energy, reduced sleep need, racing thoughts, risky behavior, elevated mood or irritability. Lasts at least 7 days.

Hypomania

A less severe form of mania — elevated mood, increased productivity. Less disruptive, but still a clinical state.

Depression

Persistent low mood, fatigue, hopelessness, cognitive fog. Often the phase most commonly experienced over a lifetime.

Mixed Features

Simultaneous mania and depression — high energy paired with low mood. Often the most distressing presentation.

The diagnostic landscape

Bipolar isn't one thing.

The spectrum includes several distinct diagnoses, each with different patterns and treatment considerations.

◈

Bipolar I

Defined by manic episodes lasting at least 7 days, or requiring hospitalization. Depressive episodes typically also occur. The most recognizable presentation, often with significant functional impact during episodes.

◇

Bipolar II

Involves hypomanic episodes and major depressive episodes — but never full mania. Often misdiagnosed as depression alone, since hypomania can feel like "finally feeling normal." Depression is usually the dominant burden.

◌

Cyclothymia

A milder, chronic pattern of hypomanic and depressive symptoms that don't fully meet criteria for either. Lasting at least 2 years, it still significantly affects relationships, work, and wellbeing.

◎

Other Specified

Bipolar patterns that don't fit neatly into the above categories — including rapid cycling (4+ episodes per year) or bipolar features triggered by substances or medical conditions.

What actually helps

There is no single right path.
But there are real ones.

Effective management of bipolar disorder is usually a combination of approaches tailored over time. What works is deeply personal.

Clinical treatments

  • Mood stabilizers (lithium, valproate, lamotrigine) — often the backbone of treatment
  • Atypical antipsychotics for acute mania or as maintenance
  • Antidepressants used carefully, often with a mood stabilizer, for depressive phases
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) adapted for bipolar disorder
  • Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT) — stabilizing daily routines
  • Family-focused therapy for strengthening support systems
  • ECT for severe, treatment-resistant depression or mania

Daily self-management

  • Consistent sleep and wake times — disrupted sleep is a major trigger
  • Mood journaling or apps to track patterns and early warning signs
  • Reducing or eliminating alcohol and recreational drugs
  • Regular physical activity, especially for depressive phases
  • Building a "wellness plan" with your care team for high-risk periods
  • Identifying personal triggers and creating protocols around them
  • Connecting with peer support groups — NAMI, DBSA, and others
